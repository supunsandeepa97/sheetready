The SheetReady Pulse

One short, practical money or small-business tip every day — budgeting, cash flow, invoicing, freelancer tax and bookkeeping, in plain English. No fluff, no jargon, newest at the top.

23 July 2026

Budget by paycheck if your bills don't line up with the month

Monthly budgets assume your money arrives in one monthly lump. If you are paid every two weeks, that mismatch causes a scramble in the weeks rent or big bills fall due.

Instead, budget each paycheck as it arrives: assign that specific paycheck to the specific bills due before the next one comes in. Two extra "third paychecks" appear most years — plan those for savings or debt in advance so they are not just absorbed.

22 July 2026

Save a little each month for the bills you know are coming

Annual costs — insurance, renewals, holidays, a tax bill — are not surprises, yet they wreck budgets because they land all at once. A sinking fund spreads them out: divide the known cost by the months until it is due and set that aside each month.

By the time the bill arrives, you have already saved for it, and it never derails the rest of your budget. Keep a line per upcoming expense so you always know how much is reserved for what.

21 July 2026

Reconcile your accounts weekly, not yearly

Waiting until tax season to match your records against your bank statement turns a small job into a dreaded one — and old mistakes are far harder to explain months later.

Block fifteen minutes once a week to confirm every transaction in your tracker matches what actually cleared the bank. Catching a duplicate or a missing entry while you still remember the purchase is the difference between a tidy book and a guessing game.

20 July 2026

Open a separate bank account for the business on day one

Mixing business and personal money in one account makes bookkeeping a forensic exercise and clouds whether the business is actually making money. A dedicated business account draws a clean line from the start.

Every business payment in and out flows through one place, so your records practically write themselves and your personal spending stays out of the picture. It is the single most useful structural habit a new business can adopt.

19 July 2026

Pay yourself a fixed amount from the business each month

When business income and personal spending share one flow, neither budget is readable. Decide on a fixed amount you will move from the business to your personal account on the same day each month, and treat it like a wage you owe yourself.

This keeps the business's real cash position honest and gives your household budget a steady, predictable number to plan around. Our cash flow guide shows how to separate the two cleanly.

18 July 2026

Start with a spreadsheet before you pay for finance software

Finance apps are powerful, but a subscription you barely use is just another expense. A spreadsheet costs nothing, forces you to understand your own numbers, and is usually enough until your volume genuinely outgrows it.

Begin simple. When you find yourself fighting the spreadsheet — too many transactions, multiple users, integrations you need — that is the signal to consider software. Until then, the sheet keeps you closer to your money.

17 July 2026

Pick the one number to check every day

Dashboards full of metrics are easy to ignore. Choosing a single number that best reflects your situation — cash on hand for a business, money left to spend this week for a household — makes a daily glance meaningful.

One clear number you actually look at beats ten you never do. Build the rest of your tracking around surfacing that number quickly, and let everything else stay one click deeper.

16 July 2026

List every subscription once a quarter and cut what you forgot

Subscriptions are designed to be invisible — small, automatic, easy to forget. The forgetting is the cost. Once a quarter, list every recurring charge against your statement and ask of each one whether you actually used it.

Cancel anything you cannot justify, and note the renewal dates of the ones you keep so the next price rise does not slip past you. This single review often pays for itself many times over.

15 July 2026

Log work travel as you go, not from memory at year-end

Work-related travel is often deductible, but reconstructing months of trips from memory is both painful and unreliable. A quick note at the time — date, distance, purpose — turns a guess into a record you can stand behind.

Whatever method your tax rules allow, the principle is the same: capture it when it happens. A running log beats a frantic estimate, and it protects you if anyone ever asks for the detail. Confirm the rules for your country.

14 July 2026

Price from your costs and target margin, not from a guess

Setting a price by gut feel or by matching a competitor ignores the only question that keeps you in business: does this price cover all your costs and leave the margin you need? Start from the cost to deliver, add your target margin, and check the result against the market.

Include the costs that are easy to forget — your own time, fees, overhead — not just materials. A price that feels fine but quietly loses money is the slowest way to fail.
